### Phasor Diagram of a Transformer
#transformers #phasor-diagram #ac-analysis

> The phasor diagram of a transformer is a graphical representation that shows the magnitude and phase relationships between the various voltages (applied, induced, terminal) and currents (primary, secondary, no-load) within the transformer. It is an essential tool for understanding the effects of load, power factor, and internal impedances on the transformer's performance, particularly its voltage regulation.

#### Construction of the Phasor Diagram (Inductive Load)
#phasor-diagram/construction

The diagram is built step-by-step, typically using the mutual flux ($\Phi$) as the common reference phasor since it links both windings. Here is the construction for a practical transformer supplying an inductive (lagging power factor) load, which is the most common scenario.

1.  **Reference Flux ($\Phi$)**: Draw the flux phasor $\Phi$ along the positive horizontal axis.
2.  **Induced EMFs ($E_1$ and $E_2$)**: The induced EMFs are generated by the changing flux. According to Faraday's law ($e = -N \frac{d\Phi}{dt}$), if $\Phi = \Phi_m \sin(\omega t)$, then $e = -N\omega\Phi_m \cos(\omega t) = N\omega\Phi_m \sin(\omega t - 90^\circ)$. Thus, both **$E_1$ and $E_2$ lag the flux $\Phi$ by 90°**. They are drawn vertically downwards and are in phase with each other.
3.  **Secondary Current ($I_2$)**: For an inductive load, the secondary current $I_2$ **lags** the secondary terminal voltage $V_2$ by the load power factor angle $\phi_2$. (Note: We don't know the exact position of $V_2$ yet, but we know this relationship holds).
4.  **Secondary Voltage Drops**:
    *   The resistive drop $I_2 R_2$ is in phase with $I_2$.
    *   The reactive drop $I_2 X_2$ leads $I_2$ by 90°.
5.  **Secondary KVL**: The secondary induced EMF $E_2$ must supply both the terminal voltage $V_2$ and the internal impedance drop.
    $$\boxed{\quad \vec{E_2} = \vec{V_2} + \vec{I_2}R_2 + j\vec{I_2}X_2 = \vec{V_2} + \vec{I_2}Z_2 \quad}$$
    This allows us to locate $V_2$ on the diagram by starting at the tip of $E_2$ and subtracting the impedance drop phasors.
6.  **No-Load Current ($I_0$)**: This current is drawn by the primary to set up the flux and supply core losses. It is the phasor sum of the magnetizing current $I_m$ (which is in phase with $\Phi$) and the core loss current $I_c$ (which is in phase with the voltage component that cancels $E_1$, i.e., $-E_1$).
7.  **Reflected Secondary Current ($I_2'$)**: The primary current must include a component to counteract the MMF of the secondary current. This is the reflected current $\vec{I_2'} = \vec{I_2}/a$. It is in phase with $I_2$.
8.  **Primary Current ($I_1$)**: The total primary current is the phasor sum of the no-load current and the reflected load current.
    $$\boxed{\quad \vec{I_1} = \vec{I_0} + \vec{I_2'} \quad}$$
9.  **Primary Voltage Drops**: Similar to the secondary, the primary winding has resistive ($I_1R_1$) and reactive ($I_1X_1$) drops.
10. **Primary KVL**: The applied primary voltage $V_1$ must overcome the primary back-EMF ($E_1$) and supply the primary impedance drop.
    $$\boxed{\quad \vec{V_1} = -\vec{E_1} + \vec{I_1}R_1 + j\vec{I_1}X_1 = -\vec{E_1} + \vec{I_1}Z_1 \quad}$$
    Here, $-\vec{E_1}$ is a phasor equal in magnitude to $E_1$ but shifted by 180°. It is drawn vertically upwards.

#### Phasor Diagrams for Different Power Factors
#power-factor

The shape of the phasor diagram changes significantly with the load power factor.
*   **Inductive Load (Lagging PF)**: As constructed above. The voltage drops tend to reduce the output voltage $V_2$ significantly.
*   **Resistive Load (Unity PF)**: $I_2$ is in phase with $V_2$. The voltage drop phasors are more perpendicular to $V_2$, resulting in a smaller overall voltage regulation.
*   **Capacitive Load (Leading PF)**: $I_2$ leads $V_2$. The reactive voltage drop can partially or completely cancel the resistive drop's effect, leading to a very small or even **negative voltage regulation** (i.e., the output voltage $V_2$ can be higher than the no-load voltage).
