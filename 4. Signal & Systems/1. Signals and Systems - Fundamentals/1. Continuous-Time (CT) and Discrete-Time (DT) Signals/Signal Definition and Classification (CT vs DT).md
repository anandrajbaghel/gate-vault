### Signal Definition and Classification (CT vs DT)
#signal-basics #signal-classification #continuous-time #discrete-time

> A **signal** is a function of one or more independent variables that conveys information about a physical phenomenon. The independent variable is typically time ($t$), but can also be space, distance, etc. The value of the function is the dependent variable, representing the signal's amplitude.

For the purpose of GATE and most engineering applications, we primarily deal with signals that are functions of a single variable: time.

#### Classification based on the Independent Variable
#continuous-time-signals #discrete-time-signals

The most fundamental classification of a signal is based on the nature of its time variable.

##### Continuous-Time (CT) Signals
#continuous-time-signals

A signal $x(t)$ is a **continuous-time signal** if the independent variable $t$ is continuous. This means the signal is defined for every instant of time over a given interval.

-   **Notation**: $x(t)$
-   **Representation**: Typically drawn as a solid line or curve.
-   **Examples**:
    -   Voltage across a resistor in an RC circuit.
    -   A speech waveform recorded by a microphone.
    -   Temperature of a room over a day.

##### Discrete-Time (DT) Signals
#discrete-time-signals

A signal $x[n]$ is a **discrete-time signal** if it is defined only at discrete instants of time. The independent variable, $n$, is an integer called the sample index.

-   **Notation**: $x[n]$ (using square brackets is the standard convention).
-   **Origin**: DT signals are most commonly generated by [[Representation of a CT signal by its Samples|sampling]] a CT signal at regular intervals. If the sampling period is $T_s$, then the relationship between continuous time and the discrete index is $t = nT_s$.
-   **Representation**: Typically drawn as a "stem" plot or "lollipop" plot to emphasize that it only has values at specific points in time.
-   **Examples**:
    -   Daily closing value of a stock market index.
    -   A digital audio signal stored on a CD.
    -   A digital image (which is a 2D discrete signal).

---
#### Classification based on the Dependent Variable (Amplitude)
#analog-signals #digital-signals

This classification is about the values the signal's amplitude can take.

-   **Analog Signal**: A signal whose amplitude can take on any value in a continuous range. Most real-world signals are analog. A CT signal is typically also an analog signal.
-   **Digital Signal**: A signal whose amplitude can only take on a finite number of discrete values. A digital signal is both discrete in time and discrete in amplitude (quantized).

The process of converting a real-world signal for digital processing is:
**Analog CT Signal** $\xrightarrow{\text{Sampling}}$ **Analog DT Signal** $\xrightarrow{\text{Quantization}}$ **Digital Signal**

#### Other Important Classifications
#signal-properties

These classifications are crucial for analyzing signals and systems. They are explored in more detail in separate notes.

1.  **Deterministic vs. Random Signals**
    -   **Deterministic**: A signal whose behavior over time is completely specified by a mathematical formula (e.g., $x(t) = A\cos(\omega t + \phi)$). There is no uncertainty.
    -   **Random (Stochastic)**: A signal whose values cannot be predicted with certainty and must be characterized by statistical properties (e.g., thermal noise in an electronic circuit).

2.  **Periodic vs. Aperiodic Signals**
    -   **Periodic**: A signal that repeats itself after a specific time interval, known as the fundamental period.
    -   **Aperiodic (or Non-periodic)**: A signal that does not repeat itself.
    -   *See [[Signal Properties (Periodic & Aperiodic, Even & Odd)]]*

3.  **Energy vs. Power Signals**
    -   This classification is based on whether the signal has finite total energy or finite average power. A signal can be one, the other, or neither, but not both.
    -   *See [[Energy and Power Signals]]*

4.  **Even vs. Odd Signals**
    -   This classification is based on the signal's symmetry with respect to the time origin ($t=0$).
    -   **Even Signal**: Symmetric about the vertical axis, $x(t) = x(-t)$.
    -   **Odd Signal**: Anti-symmetric about the origin, $x(t) = -x(-t)$.
    -   *See [[Signal Properties (Periodic & Aperiodic, Even & Odd)]]*
